My second summer interning at API we were having issues keeping filter lenses parallel to their carrier plate. The previous tooling used a clamp to apply pressure to the filter and prevent glue wicking underneath it, but the force applied caused warpage of the filter or tungsten carbide plate. I used a brass lid for the tooling to weigh down the filters, preventing wicking and warping, and maintaining the micron parallelism of the filters to the plate
This is the tooling base that accepted the lid described above. The tooling included finger holes for picking up the finished part. Making it look like a Mario face block was just fun to do

My first project at my first internship was to redesign the underperforming microscope heating chamber. The recirculating system put excessive strain on the small fan, which couldn't push enough air to provide the heat needed. My first fix was a plenum on the return side which stabilized air into the heater and restored expected performance. This wasn't the final design we went with, but it was an important step in discovering the ultimate solution
After a hose fell off while running a test with the design above, performance skyrocketed. Chasing that performance I ended up eliminating the recirculating system, and filtered the incoming air instead. This filter box held the selected filter, and attached to the heater with magnets so it could be easily removed and sanitized in laboratory use
